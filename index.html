"I Don't Trust the Township With My Money — What About Those Lawsuits?"

The most common reason people say no. Here's the complete, honest answer.

If you've heard about Baughman Township's past lawsuits and wasted money — you are right to be skeptical. Those concerns are legitimate. But there is one fact that changes everything: levy money is legally impossible to spend on lawsuits, attorneys, or anything except fire and EMS contracts. This isn't a promise. It's Ohio state law.

⚠️ What Happened (2018–2021)
In 2018, Baughman Township left the East Wayne Joint Fire District — a shared regional fire arrangement. A bitter dispute followed over how the shared savings account should be divided. The township believed it was owed far more than the County Auditor calculated ($50K–$200K vs. the auditor's ~$2,800 figure). Courts dismissed the main case in December 2021.

Those legal costs came from the township's General Fund — the regular checking account that trustees control freely. That account has no restrictions. Trustees spent it on lawsuits. That was wrong — and residents are right to be angry.
✅ Why This Levy Is Different
A fire/EMS levy creates a completely separate account — a restricted fund governed by Ohio Revised Code § 5705. Trustees have zero legal authority to transfer levy money to any other purpose.

It works exactly like the road & bridge fund: that money can only fix roads, and the State Auditor verifies it every year. The levy fund works identically. The lawsuits that happened — they are over. And they could never happen again with levy money. It is legally impossible.
⚖️
Bottom line: The lawsuits were paid from the General Fund — the same "jar" that trustees can use for anything. A levy creates a second jar with a padlock only the State of Ohio can open. No trustee — past, present, or future — can touch levy money for lawsuits, salaries, or anything non-fire/EMS. Voting No does not punish the trustees. It removes your fire truck.
💡 Think of It This Way

Imagine two jars on your counter. The first — your household checking account — you can spend on anything. The township's General Fund is that jar, and that's what paid for the lawsuits.

The second jar is labeled "FIRE TRUCKS & PARAMEDICS ONLY." It has a padlock, and only the State of Ohio holds the key. That's what this levy creates. No trustee can use it for anything else — physically, legally impossible.

"What Actually Happens If the Levy Fails?"

Ohio law is clear — and the answer may surprise you.
Is the township legally required to provide fire or EMS coverage?

Not Sure If You're in Baughman Township?

Your mailing address city is NOT the same as your township — many Orrville and Marshallville addresses are actually inside Baughman Township

⚠️

Common misconception: Many rural Wayne County residents believe that because their mailing address says "Orrville, OH" or "Marshallville, OH," they live in that city or village. That is often not the case. The U.S. Postal Service assigns mailing cities based on the nearest post office — not your political township boundary. Hundreds of Baughman Township residents have an Orrville or Marshallville mailing address but are legally and politically residents of unincorporated Baughman Township.

📮 "Orrville, OH" Mailing Address
Roads like Burkhart Rd, Church Rd, Carr Rd, Geiser Rd, and Moser Rd west of Orrville city limits carry an Orrville mailing address — but are in Baughman Township
Orrville city limits end roughly at the eastern edge of the township. Rural properties just west of that line get Orrville mail but pay Baughman Township taxes
If your parcel number at the auditor starts with 04-, you are in Baughman Township regardless of what city name is on your mail
📮 "Marshallville, OH" Mailing Address
Back Massillon Rd, Fosnight Rd, Fulton Rd, and SR 57 rural addresses near Marshallville often carry a Marshallville mailing address but lie in Baughman Township
Marshallville village itself is a small incorporated area — properties just outside those village limits on rural roads are in the township, not the village
The Marshallville Fire Department is physically near the township — but township residents are not automatically covered by the village fire district

"Why Does the Township Suddenly Need Money?" — The Full Story

Artificially low fire rates + a one-time estate tax windfall that is now gone. The math finally caught up.
For years, Baughman Township residents paid remarkably little for fire protection compared to every neighboring township. That wasn't good management — it was an accident of history. Two forces kept costs artificially low: billing errors by contracted departments that undercharged the township for years, and a steady stream of estate tax revenue that Ohio has since repealed. Both are now gone. The bill has come due.
🧾

Artificially Low Fire Department Billing

Cause 1 of 2 · Now Corrected

For a significant period, the contracted fire departments undercharged Baughman Township — either through billing errors, outdated flat-rate agreements that were never renegotiated, or administrative oversights on their end. The township paid those low rates and built its budget around them.

When departments began auditing and correcting their contracts — and one major department signaled it would require full renegotiation — the true cost of fire coverage became clear for the first time.

Current contracts run approximately $60,000 per department per year — $180,000 total — and are projected to increase to $198,000 by Year 3. One department has indicated it will shift to per-call billing at ~$1,100/call if the levy fails, which could easily exceed $300,000+ annually depending on call volume.

Bottom line: The township was living on a discount that was never guaranteed to last. Now it has ended — and the real cost of fire protection is on the table.
🏦

The Estate Tax "Piggy Bank" — Gone

Cause 2 of 2 · Ohio Repealed 2013

Ohio's estate tax — a tax on inherited wealth above a threshold, paid by estates of deceased residents — was a significant and unpredictable source of township revenue for decades. Townships and counties received a share of estate tax collections from residents who died in their jurisdiction.

In Wayne County generally, and in Baughman Township specifically, estate tax windfalls periodically padded the General Fund with tens of thousands — or in strong years, over $100,000 — in one-time revenue. Township budgets were written with the expectation that this money would continue flowing.

Ohio repealed the estate tax effective January 1, 2013. The last payments trickled in through the mid-2010s as estates settled. By the late 2010s, the pipeline had fully dried up — and the General Fund deficits that began in 2020 are directly traceable to life without that revenue cushion.

Bottom line: The "piggy bank" funded years of below-levy operations. Ohio took the piggy bank away in 2013. The township spent the next decade slowly drawing down the savings — and now they are gone.

💬 Plain English: What This Means for You

Baughman Township didn't suddenly need a fire levy because of mismanagement or reckless spending. The township operated for years on two artificial advantages: fire departments that were undercharging them and a state estate tax that sent regular windfalls to the General Fund.

Both of those advantages are gone now — one corrected by the departments, the other eliminated by the Ohio legislature in 2013. The township spent roughly a decade drawing down the savings those windfalls created. That buffer is now exhausted.

The levy isn't a request for more money than the township needs. It's a request for a stable, honest source of funding for a service the township has always depended on — but has never had to pay the real price for until now. Every other township in Wayne County already pays this levy. Baughman is simply being asked to catch up to what everyone else has been doing for years.

🔥 How the Fire & EMS Levy Is Calculated

Ohio taxes property on 35% of appraised value — not the full market price.

Step 1: Appraised Value × 35% = Assessed Value
$135,900 × 35% = $47,565

Step 2: Assessed Value × Levy Rate ÷ 1,000 = Annual Tax
$47,565 × 1.75 ÷ 1,000 = $83.24/year

Step 3: Divide by 12 for monthly cost
$83.24 ÷ 12 = $6.94/month
$83.24 per year $6.94 / month
23¢ per day

📐 The Ohio Property Tax Formula

Appraised Value × 35%
= Assessed Value

Assessed Value × 1.75 ÷ 1,000
= Annual Fire Levy Cost
Why 35%? Ohio law (ORC § 5713.03) requires all real property to be assessed at 35% of its "true value" for tax purposes. This "assessed value" is the taxable base — not your full market value.

Agricultural land uses the CAUV (Current Agricultural Use Valuation) method, which typically results in a lower assessed value. Contact the Wayne County Auditor for your exact CAUV figure.
